Introduction

Weather documentation is important for several reasons:

  • It tells you the difference between hail and sleet
  • It reduces the odds that I will get mad at you for calling sleet "hail"
  • It relieves high blood pressure caused by people saying "it's hailing!" in the middle of January, which would ordinarily be cause for concern -- as it should never hail in January -- but it turns out the people are simply using the word "hail" incorrectly
The National Weather Service issues alerts for severe weather, which used to be in all caps:

NATIONAL WEATHER SERVICE!

The alerts are no longer in all caps, but for nostalgia's sake, and to fulfill the outlines of this web assignment, alert text will be rendered in monospace all caps.

Snow

If at least 6 inches of snow is forecast to fall, a "Winter Storm" alert will be issued by the National Weather Service. A "Watch" means that there is medium confidence that the storm will hit soon. A "Warning" means there is high confidence that the storm will hit imminently or that the storm is in progress hitting an area.

WINTER STORM WARNING / WINTER STORM WATCH

But sometimes, only a few inches of snow will fall.

WINTER WEATHER ADVISORY

And sometimes, the amount of snow that will accumulate is negligible and no specific advisories are issued. Instead, depending on the local National Weather Service office, a general advisory may be issued.

HAZARDOUS WEATHER OUTLOOK

Thunderstorm

Thunderstorms produce some or all of the following:

  • Lightning (accompanied by thunder)
  • Hail
  • High Winds
  • Rain
Thunderstorms are known for their tall cumulonimbus clouds.

SEVERE THUNDERSTORM WARNING / SEVERE THUNDERSTORM WATCH

If a thunderstorm is expected to produce a tornado, additional alerts will be issued:

TORNADO WARNING / TORNADO WATCH

Hail vs Sleet

This item usually would not get its own top-level header, but for purposes of practicing code, it gets the full header treatment now!

Hail and sleet are two fundamentally different concepts. Some people call any ice falling from the sky "hail" but in doing so, they make a fundamental error that affects emergency preparedness efforts.

Hail:

  • Usually falls during a thunderstorm
  • Can grow to a large size before falling
  • Is formed when a water droplet is forced upward into a tall thundercloud and freezes. As it moves higher, more water freezes to it, making it larger. It drops after reaching a weight greater than the thundercloud can support.
  • Can be harmful to life and property
Sleet:
  • Falls during a "wintry mix" event, when there is precipitation with temperatures around freezing
  • Is a constant size -- a small ice pellet
  • Is formed when snow melts and refreezes, or rain freezes, on its way down (note that if it does not refreeze until making contact with an object, it is instead considered "freezing rain")
  • Is almost always harmless to life and property

You will sometimes see hail during a severe thunderstorm:

SEVERE THUNDERSTORM WARNING

You will usually see sleet during a wintry mix, which is sometimes accompanied by a winter weather advisory.

WINTER WEATHER ADVISORY
